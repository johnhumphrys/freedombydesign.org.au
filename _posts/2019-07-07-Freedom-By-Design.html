It has been yet another huge weekend for our very special taxi… Beth the Social Enterprise Taxi. We always meet great people who visit the beautiful Yarra Valley. It is lovely to share in their weekend away and help them relax into the Valley’s plentiful places for indulgent venues because they know we will get them home! In many ways, Beth has become a part of the array of attractions in the Yarra Valley. It warms our hearts when tourists get in Beth and talk about how many businesses and people highly recommend us.

The spirit of Beth is the center from which we provide a taxi service. We often explain to people that we are not taxi drivers, but we drive a taxi. We are not degrading other people who drive taxis; far from it. We have a huge appreciation of them and the work they do. Having said that, the taxi industry doesn’t have a great reputation overall.

Of course, we are part of the community. We love catching up with our locals who are just not passengers but our supporters and friends. It feels amazing when we get a phone call from someone telling us we have a taillight out, have offers of support and encouragement.

Building the Social Enterprise has become a labour of love and has always thrown challenges and cur balls in both business and personal sense. But it’s so easy to fall in love with the spirit of Beth and share that spirit with every person we meet along the way.

‘It will get easier to put yourself out there Jen… just keep doing it and you will become good at it’

... They were wrong!

I write this from a deeply personal place; something I struggle sharing with others.

I grew up in a small country town called Merrigum. Reflecting back, I’m grateful for the safeness and innocents I grew up with. In a sense, I am still naïve and struggle to make sense of many things. I was painfully shy kid who isn’t great socially… I still am in my 50s!

I went to university when I was 35. I had never really completed anything during my life and decided that it would be really cool to have a degree. I took off when I was 15 to work on a horse stud (my life dream). I dropped out of school right before the year 10 exams. I knew then, and I know now that I’m not great at applying myself; I have the ability but don’t put in the effort. To me, my time was not ill spent. I’d daydream of my perfect life… spent a lot of time trying to figure out how people ticked… watched what was going on around me… and trying really hard to do right by people.

I never had the strength to stand up for myself properly. I guess most of us would identify with… how do I stand up for myself and my beliefs without offending or hurting someone. A lot of us would also identify with internalising the hurt or injustice… and then trying to deal with it. For me it has always been abusing food, lack of self-care and avoidance. This combined with shyness.. crikies!!!

I remember when I was a kid. The doctor said can you take tablets, or do you need medicine? Not wanting to question authority, I said, yep I can take tablets. I was wrong. A strange reflection perhaps. But really, it’s just an example of a lifelong ability to bite off more than I could chew!

Predictable enough, my 15 year old horse stud experience didn’t go so well. The picture in my mind of Mum coming and ‘rescuing’ me after a year is forever close to my heart. I didn’t realise at the time because I was so starstruck at my animal career that I wasn’t treated very well. I’m glad Mum did. I was a bit broken.

Over the next few years I loved my time in the dairy industry… but that’s a whole other story. I decided to go to night school and do Year 12. I loved it and got great grades. I had the study bug!

My hubby at the time wasn’t interested in farming. I ended up living in the Melbourne suburb, Bayswater! I rehomed my horses and cows. I wasn’t unhappy with the idea and have no regrets about moving to Melbourne.

When we moved to Melbourne, I went to TAFE and studied to become a youth worker… that’s another story. I worked as an integration aide with a young man who struggled socially at primary school. We became foster parents and various kids come and go. I worked as a notetaker at TAFE and started doing my degree in Applied Science (disability studies) part time.

One of the people I took notes for at TAFE lived in a group home. I just couldn’t believe what I was hearing. Happily living in my daydream bubble, I had no idea that such places existed; it sounded awful.

I figured that to be able to understand what group homes are and how they work, I should experience it. I took a group home team leader role with a large Melbourne organisation.

It was an interesting time. The shift from institutionalisation to Normalisation thinking began. Individualisation and the social model of support had begun. Institutions/Group homes had a name change. They become Community Residential Units… then Supported Living Houses… then they went back to being called Group Homes. Individual Person Plans became known as Person Centered Plans… and on it goes…

It was interesting to be at uni and learn the theory behind what goes on within the ‘disability sector’. But really… nothing made sense to me. People are people. Why do well-meaning people further disable other people? What makes a person think that they know what is right for another person? Why do people hide behind legislation in an attempt to justify their actions towards vulnerable people?

On a personal level, this was a really challenging time. I went on a personal quest with a psychologist to figure out who am I… and why am I the way I am? I never really figured it out. When questioning what I saw and what I was expected to do within Group Home and foster care settings, it never ended well. It became clear to me that initialisation is a mindset; a force to be reckoned with. It breaks people!

I went in search of a solution and ended up in America on a study tour for my uni placement. Me in Los Angeles.. good heavens. It all become clear!

I spent the next few years trying to get Freedom by Design Inc. off the ground to provide an alternative for people who are unhappy living in an institutionalised setting.

I’ve always been quite open that I feel my biggest challenge is getting out of my own way. That might sound weird… but that shy, socially challenged, internalising kid is still very much present.

Again, I looked for answers. I couldn’t believe it when I gained a scholarship to The School for Social Entrepreneurs; an amazing school for community members who want to help make change happen. Part of this was to develop learning goals… ‘I’m shy, I’m broke… I’ll drive a taxi’

If you’ve read this far… thank you!

I plan to share my story a bit… the good, the bad and the ugly. I’m hoping it will help make a bit more of a change in regard to how people are treated. I also hope that it will help me gather the strength and support to finish what I started in founding Freedom by Design Inc. (FBD).

Ohh… if you’re wondering about the earlier picture of my tattoo…

I promised myself that I would give up if FBD wasn’t funded by the time I turned 50. My 3 gorgeous sisters shouted me a birthday tattoo instead!!!
